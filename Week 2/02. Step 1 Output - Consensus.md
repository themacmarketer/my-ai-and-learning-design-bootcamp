## Detailed research summary

### Area 1 – Adult learning design for technical tools

**Cognitive load & scaffolding**

- Simulation‑style, tool-based environments are most effective when they combine **worked examples early**, then gradually shift to more open problem‑solving as prior knowledge grows; novices benefit more from examples than from pure reflection phases  (Chernikova et al., 2020).  
- Effective data literacy training uses **structured instructional support**, authentic datasets, and clear decision-making models rather than only teaching analysis procedures  (Sandoval-Ríos et al., 2025; Henderson & Corry, 2020).  
- Asynchronous video plus **short interactive exercises, peer discussion, quizzes, and individualized feedback** in ~6‑hour blocks significantly improved data-based decision-making skills  (Sandoval-Ríos et al., 2025). This supports chunking a 2‑day workshop into short concept bursts followed by practice.
- Investigative labs that ask learners to make decisions (not just “click to verify a concept”) increase engagement and conceptual understanding  (Smith & Holmes, 2021). For GA4/GTM this implies tasks like “diagnose why event X is missing” rather than only “create a report like this”.

**Mixed‑seniority groups**

- In data literacy and learning analytics contexts, **collaborative group work** and team‑based analysis tasks equalize prior differences and support shared sense‑making; post‑tests often show initial education‑level gaps disappearing after well-structured training  (Sandoval-Ríos et al., 2025; Henderson & Corry, 2020).  
- Design‑as‑inquiry models where participants collaboratively define questions and interpret data promote professional agency and more transfer into practice  (Sandoval-Ríos et al., 2025; Shibani et al., 2020). For mixed seniority, this means cross‑role teams solving real GovTech questions rather than rank‑segregated activities.

**Hands‑on ratios in intensive formats**

- Effective data‑literacy and analytics trainings range from 1–2 days (~7.5 h) to semester‑long courses, but consistently combine **brief lectures, guided exercises, and small‑group tasks**  (Sandoval-Ríos et al., 2025).  
- Across simulation‑based learning in higher education, large effects (g≈0.85) are reported when learners spend substantial time *doing* within the system; scaffolding via examples and prompts is crucial, especially for novices  (Chernikova et al., 2020).  
- Synthesizing these, a 2‑day technical workshop for novices is best run at roughly **25–35% instructor input / demo and 65–75% guided practice and collaborative problem‑solving**, with tasks increasing in complexity.

### Area 2 – GA4/GTM instructional design

**Best‑practice sequences (from GA4 education literature and learning‑analytics design)**

- For beginners, a recommended sequence is:  
  1) Core concepts (events, parameters, users, sessions),  
  2) Navigating standard GA4 reports,  
  3) Defining business questions and KPIs,  
  4) Custom events and conversions,  
  5) Exploration workspace and funnels,  
  6) Tagging and configuration via GTM,  
  7) Governance and data quality checks  (McGuirk, 2023; Gueye & Expósito, 2024; Lee et al., 2020).  
- Learning‑analytics‑oriented instructional design stresses **starting from questions and outcomes**, then mapping which data and events are actually needed, to avoid indiscriminate data collection  (Gueye & Expósito, 2024). This aligns well with GA4/GTM planning before implementation.

**Common misconceptions & errors (and how to pre‑empt)**

From GA4 platform reviews and learning‑analytics practice  (McGuirk, 2023; Lee et al., 2020; Gueye & Expósito, 2024):

- Treating GA4 as “UA with a new interface”: misunderstanding the **event‑based model**, leading to incorrect expectations about metrics and reports  (McGuirk, 2023).  
  - Pre‑empt by explicitly contrasting UA vs GA4 conceptual model, using side‑by‑side examples.  
- Over‑collecting data: logging many events/parameters without mapping to learning or business outcomes; this burdens analysis and governance  (Gueye & Expósito, 2024).  
  - Pre‑empt with an “event design canvas” activity where each event is linked to a stakeholder question and decision.  
- Focusing solely on “page views / sessions” instead of **task‑ or outcome‑oriented events**; akin to learning‑analytics projects that hoard LMS clicks rather than aligning traces with learning outcomes  (Lee et al., 2020; Gueye & Expósito, 2024).  
  - Pre‑empt by starting with use cases (e.g., “measure campaign X registrations”) and back‑designing implementation.  
- Confusion around sampling, attribution, and differences between real‑time vs standard reports  (McGuirk, 2023).  
  - Pre‑empt by a guided “read this report and explain what it *doesn’t* tell you” exercise.

GTMs role is infrastructural but the same instructional principle applies: collect only **relevant, interpretable traces** linked to outcomes  (Gueye & Expósito, 2024).

### Area 3 – Government/public sector digital capability

**Digital capability and performance**

- In a 2024 study of a Justice ministry, **data‑driven decision making and analytics capabilities** significantly improved organizational efficiency and service quality, with **organizational agility mediating over half the performance impact**  (Atobishi et al., 2024). Training must therefore be coupled with process and culture changes, not just tool skills.
- AI and analytics capabilities in municipalities improve performance through **process automation and cognitive insight**, but poorly integrated “cognitive engagement” initiatives can hurt performance if they outpace organizational readiness  (Mikalef et al., 2023). This reinforces the need to align GA4/GTM training with realistic use cases and support structures.

**Barriers and what makes training stick**

- E‑government ICT training for citizens significantly boosts **self‑efficacy and digital literacy**, helping bridge the digital divide  (Chohan & Hu, 2020). However, effectiveness depends on contextualization to users’ real tasks.  
- Studies on government digital champions show that barriers are often **cultural and structural**, and that successful change agents rely on: storytelling with citizen‑impact narratives, community building, external validation, and visible leadership support  (Wilson & Mergel, 2022).  
- Reviews of government and MSME digital training highlight low post‑training implementation when there is a **gap between training content and real work needs**, lack of ongoing mentoring, and limited follow‑up incentives  (Selvi et al., 2025).  
- Learning‑analytics adoption in education indicates that **co‑design with practitioners**, explicit attention to educator/participant agency, and integrating tools into existing workflows increase sustained use  (Shibani et al., 2020).

Implications for your workshop:

- Tie every GA4/GTM exercise to real GovTech or public‑service scenarios.  
- Involve managers/directors in defining a small set of **post‑workshop analytics commitments** (e.g., a shared dashboard and review cadence).  
- Provide follow‑up support or communities of practice, not just a standalone 2‑day event  (Shibani et al., 2020; Wilson & Mergel, 2022; Selvi et al., 2025).

## Step‑by‑step “how to” guide (beginner‑friendly)

This is framed as a learning journey you can adapt directly into your 2‑day plan.

### Step 1 – Frame meaningful questions and KPIs

1. In mixed groups, list 3–5 real questions (e.g., “Which channels drive sign‑ups for Event X?”).  
2. Translate each into 1–2 **measurable outcomes** (registrations, downloads, contact form submissions).  
3. Decide who will use the data and what decisions they will make.  
   - Mirrors data‑driven decision‑making models shown to improve practical use of analytics  (Sandoval-Ríos et al., 2025; Henderson & Corry, 2020; Chernikova et al., 2020; Gueye & Expósito, 2024).

### Step 2 – Understand the GA4 “mental model”

1. Explain (with visuals, not code) that GA4 is **event‑based**, not session/pageview‑centric  (McGuirk, 2023).  
2. Show how an action (click “Register”) becomes an event with parameters in GA4.  
3. Contrast one example report in UA vs GA4 to surface differences  (McGuirk, 2023).  
4. Quick quiz where participants match scenarios to “event” vs “parameter” vs “user property”.

### Step 3 – Navigate core GA4 reports

1. Guided tour: Home, Reports snapshot, Acquisition, Engagement, Demographics, Tech  (McGuirk, 2023).  
2. For 2–3 of the earlier questions, ask: “Which standard report gives a partial answer?”  
3. Short exercise: change date ranges, add comparisons, and save a simple report collection.  
   - Reflective phases are more effective for those with some prior knowledge, so keep this brief early on  (Chernikova et al., 2020).

### Step 4 – Design your event and data plan (no code)

1. Introduce a simple **event design canvas**: Question → Outcome → Event name → Parameters → Audience.  
2. In groups, design 3–5 events per website/app aligned to your earlier KPIs  (Gueye & Expósito, 2024).  
3. Label which need **GTM** vs which may be auto‑collected (enhanced measurement).  
4. Sanity‑check: remove any event that no one will ever use for a decision  (Lee et al., 2020; Gueye & Expósito, 2024).

### Step 5 – Introduction to GTM concepts

1. Explain tags, triggers, variables using **metaphors** (e.g., “If X happens, send a postcard to GA4”).  
2. Show a read‑only container: identify where GA4 config tag lives, how a click trigger is set.  
3. Hands‑on in a sandbox: duplicate a simple tag, change the event name only.  
   - This corresponds to low‑stakes, example‑supported practice for novices  (Chernikova et al., 2020).

### Step 6 – Implement a basic event (guided)

1. Starting from one designed event (“event_registration”), walk through:  
   - Create a click trigger.  
   - Create a GA4 event tag using that trigger.  
   - Preview/debug and confirm the event fires.  
2. Learners repeat for a second event following a checklist.  
3. Use a “spot the bug” challenge: show a misconfigured trigger and ask groups to fix it.  
   - Error‑focused tasks help address common misconceptions systematically  (Van Der Heyden et al., 2025; Chernikova et al., 2020).

### Step 7 – Mark conversions and build simple explorations

1. In GA4, mark 1–2 key events as conversions.  
2. Create at least one **Exploration** (funnel or path) answering a high‑priority question  (McGuirk, 2023).  
3. Ask each group to produce a one‑slide “story” from their exploration: what changed, why it matters.  
   - Supports decision‑oriented data literacy, not just report-building  (Sandoval-Ríos et al., 2025; Henderson & Corry, 2020; Chernikova et al., 2020).

### Step 8 – Governance, ethics, and documentation

1. Discuss limits: data minimization, privacy, alignment with public‑sector values  (Atobishi et al., 2024; Mikalef et al., 2023; Wilson & Mergel, 2022).  
2. Create a shared **tracking specification** template: event name, description, owner, decisions supported, retention period  (Gueye & Expósito, 2024).  
3. Agree basic roles: who changes GTM, who approves, who reviews dashboards.

### Step 9 – Plan post‑workshop use

1. Each team writes a 4‑week **action plan**:  
   - 1–2 GA4 questions to review weekly.  
   - One process they will change based on data.  
2. Identify “digital champions” per team and a community‑of‑practice channel  (Atobishi et al., 2024; Mikalef et al., 2023; Shibani et al., 2020; Wilson & Mergel, 2022).  
3. Schedule a 4–6 week check‑in to review what data was actually used  (Chohan & Hu, 2020; Selvi et al., 2025).

## Exemplars and common errors

### Exemplars (good practices)

- **Exemplar 1 – Campaign tracking for a public event**  
  - Question: which channels bring citizens to register?  
  - Design: one “register” event with source/medium and content parameters; tagged via GTM on the confirmation page.  
  - Use: weekly GA4 exploration comparing conversion rates by channel; shift spend accordingly  (McGuirk, 2023; Gueye & Expósito, 2024).

- **Exemplar 2 – Self‑service dashboard for a program team**  
  - Question: are visitors engaging with key information pages?  
  - Design: engagement events (scroll, outbound link) for a specific content group.  
  - Use: simple Looker Studio or GA4 report shared at monthly stand‑ups; stakeholders make content changes and track impact  (Atobishi et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2020).

### Common errors & how to prevent them

| Error | Why it happens | Prevention strategy | Citations |
|------|----------------|---------------------|-----------|
| Treating GA4 like UA | Mental model remains session/page‑view‑centric. | Start with explicit conceptual comparison and event mapping exercise. |  (McGuirk, 2023; Lee et al., 2020)|
| Logging too many events | “Collect everything” mindset, no link to decisions. | Event design canvas tied to concrete questions; peer critique of event plans. |  (Lee et al., 2020; Gueye & Expósito, 2024)|
| No ownership/governance | Tool seen as IT’s job only. | Define roles and champions; align with digital‑capability and agility goals. |  (Atobishi et al., 2024; Mikalef et al., 2023; Wilson & Mergel, 2022)|
| Post‑workshop drop‑off | Training not embedded in workflows; no follow‑up. | Co‑design action plans, follow‑up sessions, and peer networks. |  (Chohan & Hu, 2020; Shibani et al., 2020; Wilson & Mergel, 2022; Selvi et al., 2025)|
| Over‑reliance on single metric | Dashboards focus on vanity metrics. | Require each group to define at least one behavior/change per metric. |  (Sandoval-Ríos et al., 2025; Henderson & Corry, 2020; Chernikova et al., 2020)|

  
**Consolidated Research Brief**

2-Day GA4 & GTM Workshop for GovTech

# **1\. Executive Summary**

This brief consolidates three streams of peer-reviewed and evidence-based research to inform the instructional design of a 2-day GA4 and GTM workshop for GovTech Singapore. The audience is mixed-role (branding, marketing, events staff, plus executives, managers, and directors) and non-coding. Research draws on 15+ academic sources spanning adult learning theory, cognitive load theory, data literacy training, simulation-based learning, GA4/GTM platform pedagogy, and public-sector digital capability.

## **Four headline findings**

1. **Manage three types of cognitive load deliberately.** Simplify intrinsic load (GA4’s event model) by teaching conceptual frameworks before the interface. Minimise extraneous load (GTM’s complex UI) through scaffolded walkthroughs. Maximise germane load (building mental models of data flow) through question-first design.

2. **30–50% input, 50–70% practice.** Meta-analyses report large learning gains (g≈0.85) when learners spend the majority of time doing. Sources range from 30/70 to 50/50; for a non-coding government audience, the sweet spot is approximately 40% input/demo and 60% guided practice, with complexity increasing across both days.

3. **Question-first, tool-second.** Effective GA4/GTM training starts from real business or service-delivery questions and works backwards to which data, events, and reports answer them – never from feature tours.

4. **Post-workshop transfer is the bottleneck.** Government training studies consistently report low implementation when training is disconnected from workflows, lacks follow-up, or has no champion structure. In the public sector, compliance and privacy hurdles – not cost – are the primary barriers. Build action plans, review cadences, and “curiosity velocity” into Day 2\.

# **2\. Research Synthesis by Theme**

## **2.1 Adult Learning Design for Technical Tools**

**Cognitive load theory applied to GA4/GTM**

Training must address three types of cognitive load:

* **Intrinsic load** (the inherent difficulty of GA4’s event-based architecture) – simplify by teaching the conceptual framework before the interface. Beginners should not touch the UI until the logic is understood.

* **Extraneous load** (navigating the complex GTM interface) – can cause interface fatigue. Minimise through pre-configured sandboxes, step-by-step checklists, and clear “Why → What → How” module structures.

* **Germane load** (building mental models of data flow) – maximise through question-first design, event design canvases, and investigative tasks that require decisions (e.g. “diagnose why event X is missing”).

Sources: Chernikova et al. 2020; Sandoval-Ríos et al. 2025; Smith & Holmes 2021\.

**Scaffolding for mixed-seniority groups**

* Scaffolding should be temporary, role-specific, and aimed at maximising access to role-equivalent tasks. For practitioners: step-by-step guides for tag deployment. For executives: sentence frames for data interpretation and high-level conceptual maps.

* Collaborative group work and team-based analysis tasks equalise prior differences; post-tests often show initial education-level gaps disappearing after well-structured training (Sandoval-Ríos et al. 2025; Henderson & Corry 2020).

* **Reverse mentoring** – where junior staff guide senior staff on tech execution – and peer-led learning are highly effective strategies for bridging seniority gaps.

* Design-as-inquiry models where participants collaboratively define questions and interpret data promote professional agency and transfer into practice (Shibani et al. 2020). Cross-role teams solving real GovTech questions are preferable to rank-segregated activities.

**Hands-on ratios in intensive formats**

* Meta-analyses of simulation-based learning report large effect sizes (g≈0.85) when learners spend substantial time doing within the system, with scaffolding via worked examples and prompts crucial for novices (Chernikova et al. 2020).

* Data literacy reviews recommend 25–35% instructor input and 65–75% guided practice (Sandoval-Ríos et al. 2025). Complementary research on non-technical audiences recommends closer to 50/50 lecture-to-practice for intensive formats.

* Short, focused programmes (12–20 hours over 2–4 days) significantly improve confidence and applied skills, especially with mixed-ability cohorts (HDSR 2020).

**Consolidated recommendation:** For a non-coding government audience, aim for approximately 40% instructor input/demo and 60% guided practice with scaffolded complexity, noting that Day 1 may skew more toward input (concepts) and Day 2 more toward practice (implementation). Facilitators must normalise the discomfort of active learning – cognitive effort often feels like frustration before it becomes confidence.

## **2.2 GA4/GTM Instructional Sequence**

**Consolidated learning sequence**

Merging both recommended sequences, the consolidated progression for beginners is:

5. Conceptual framework: events, parameters, users, sessions – explicitly contrasting the UA session/pageview model with GA4’s event-based architecture.

6. Account and container architecture: GA4 property setup, web data streams, GTM container structure, and the relationship between them.

7. Navigating standard GA4 reports: Home, Acquisition, Engagement, Demographics, Tech – matched to real business questions from Step 1\.

8. Defining business questions and KPIs: event design canvas mapping Question → Outcome → Event name → Parameters → Audience.

9. GTM fundamentals: tags, triggers, variables – taught through metaphors and sandbox exploration. Create constant variable for Measurement ID.

10. Event configuration and implementation: custom events via GTM, naming conventions (snake\_case), custom parameters, registering custom definitions in GA4.

11. Key events (conversions), explorations, and funnels: marking conversions, building Exploration reports, producing insight stories.

12. Installation, verification, and debugging: GTM Preview Mode, GA4 DebugView, Tag Assistant, Incognito testing, the critical “Publish” step.

13. Governance, data quality, and documentation: tracking specifications, roles, data minimisation, retention settings (14 months), consent compliance.

Sources: McGuirk 2023; Gueye & Exposíto 2024; Lee et al. 2020; LovesData; Carnegie Higher Ed.

**Common misconceptions and pre-emption strategies**

| Misconception | Why it persists | Pre-emption strategy | Sources |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **GA4 \= UA with a new UI** | Mental model stays session/pageview-centric | Explicit UA vs GA4 conceptual comparison with side-by-side examples before touching the interface | McGuirk 2023 |
| **Category/Action/Label still applies** | Legacy UA event structure deeply ingrained | Show how GA4 replaces this with flexible event names \+ custom parameters; hands-on mapping exercise | GA4/GTM training literature |
| **Collect everything** | No link between events and decisions | Event design canvas: each event mapped to a stakeholder question and decision | Gueye & Exposíto 2024; Lee et al. 2020 |
| **Key Events ≠ Conversions** | Terminology change causes confusion | Explicitly teach that Key Events are the new name for conversions; show the UI relabelling | GA4 documentation 2024–25 |
| **Pageview/session fixation** | Legacy UA habits; analogous to hoarding LMS clicks | Start from use cases and back-design implementation around outcome-oriented events | Lee et al. 2020; Gueye & Exposíto 2024 |
| **Misreading reports (sampling, attribution)** | Insufficient conceptual grounding | Guided exercise: “Read this report and explain what it doesn’t tell you” | McGuirk 2023 |

**Technical best practices to teach**

* Establish strict naming conventions early: snake\_case event names, clear prefixes indicating track type and specific page.

* One GTM container per website to avoid cross-contamination.

* Create a Constant Variable for the GA4 Measurement ID – prevents tracking errors across multiple tags.

* Test in Incognito mode to bypass ad blockers and privacy extensions that block GTM preview.

* Explicitly teach the “Publish” step – unsubmitted workspaces are the most common cause of missing data for beginners.

* Warn against using the “Create Event” feature in GA4 whilst simultaneously using GTM – causes data duplication.

* Extend data retention from the default 2 months to 14 months (Admin → Data Settings → Data Retention).

* Register custom parameters under Custom Definitions in GA4 or they will not appear in reports.

## **2.3 Government/Public-Sector Digital Capability**

**Adoption barriers specific to government**

* Unlike the private sector, compliance and legal hurdles (privacy, data sovereignty) are the primary barriers to scaling analytics, not cost (Atobishi et al. 2024; Mikalef et al. 2023).

* Additional barriers include lack of data literacy, cultural resistance to change, and poorly integrated cognitive-engagement initiatives that can hurt performance if they outpace organisational readiness.

* E-government ICT training significantly boosts self-efficacy and digital literacy, but effectiveness depends on contextualisation to real tasks (Chohan & Hu 2020).

**What makes training stick in government**

* Data-driven decision-making capabilities significantly improve organisational efficiency and service quality, with organisational agility mediating over half the performance impact. Training must be coupled with process and culture changes, not just tool skills (Atobishi et al. 2024).

* Successful government digital champions rely on storytelling with citizen-impact narratives, community building, external validation, and visible leadership support (Wilson & Mergel 2022).

* **Curiosity velocity** – the speed at which an employee can explore data, ask questions, and convert insights into action – is a leading indicator of post-workshop success. Cultivate it through contextual learning grounded in actual agency data and workflows, not generic examples.

* Managerial involvement and fostering a culture of psychological safety where staff can “fail early and scale fast” are critical for long-term capability building.

* Low post-training implementation occurs when there is a gap between training content and real work needs, lack of ongoing mentoring, and limited follow-up incentives (Selvi et al. 2025).

* Co-design with practitioners, explicit attention to participant agency, and integrating tools into existing workflows increase sustained use (Shibani et al. 2020).

**Implications for this workshop**

* Tie every GA4/GTM exercise to real GovTech or public-service scenarios (e.g. Prompt Royale registrations, Innovation Sparks product clicks, Digital Week campaigns).

* Use actual agency data and workflows wherever possible – not generic demo accounts.

* Involve managers and directors in defining a small set of post-workshop analytics commitments (e.g. shared dashboard, review cadence).

* Provide follow-up support or communities of practice – not a standalone 2-day event. Schedule a 4–6 week check-in.

* Address compliance and consent requirements explicitly in the workshop (consent banners, data retention, data minimisation).

# **4\. Nine-Step Learning Journey**

This consolidated sequence synthesises all three research inputs into a single progression from questions to sustainable GA4/GTM use.

| Step | Activity | What participants do | Evidence rationale |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **1** | **Frame questions & KPIs** | Mixed groups list 3–5 real GovTech questions (e.g. “Which channels drive sign-ups for Prompt Royale?”), translate to measurable outcomes, define who uses the data and what decisions they make | Anchors in authentic contexts; data-driven decision-making models improve practical use. Addresses intrinsic cognitive load by establishing “why” before “how”. |
| **2** | **GA4 mental model \+ architecture** | Visually explain event-based model; contrast UA vs GA4 with side-by-side examples. Explain Category/Action/Label is obsolete. Cover GA4 property setup, web data streams, Measurement ID. Quiz on event vs parameter vs user property. | Reduces persistent UA-era misconceptions. Concepts before interface minimises extraneous cognitive load. |
| **3** | **Navigate core reports** | Guided tour of Home, Acquisition, Engagement, Demographics, Tech. Match earlier questions to reports; change date ranges, add comparisons. Exercise: “What does this report not tell you?” | Quick wins build confidence. Keep brief for novices – reflection more effective later. Normalise productive struggle. |
| **4** | **Design event & data plan** | Event design canvas: Question → Outcome → Event name → Parameters → Audience. Groups design 3–5 events per scenario; label GTM vs auto-collected; establish naming conventions (snake\_case); remove unused events via peer critique. | Prevents data overload; focuses traces on decisions. Establishes technical rigour early. |
| **5** | **GTM concepts \+ global config** | Tags, triggers, variables via metaphors (“If X happens, send a postcard to GA4”). Enable built-in variables. Create Constant Variable for Measurement ID. Configure base Google Tag with Initialisation trigger. Read-only container walkthrough. | Low-code scaffolding; example-supported practice. Constant Variable prevents errors across multiple tags. |
| **6** | **Implement events** | Guided: create click trigger (e.g. “Register” button) → GA4 event tag (snake\_case name) → add custom parameters → register under Custom Definitions. Repeat for second event with checklist. “Spot the bug” challenge on misconfigured trigger. | Scaffolded hands-on; error-focused tasks build self-diagnosis skills. Teaches that custom params need registration. |
| **7** | **Key events, explorations & insight stories** | Mark key events as conversions. Build at least one Exploration (funnel or path). Each group produces a one-slide insight story: what changed, why it matters, what to do next. | Links analysis to decisions. Key Events \= Conversions explicitly taught. Decision-oriented data literacy. |
| **8** | **Testing, debugging & publishing** | GTM Preview Mode, GA4 DebugView, Tag Assistant. Test in Incognito. Accept cookies in preview window. Explicitly teach the Publish step with descriptive version names. Troubleshoot “Peview Mode not working” scenarios. | Never publish blindly. Addresses the most common beginner errors: ad blockers, consent banners, unpublished workspaces, curly quotes from copy-paste. |
| **9** | **Governance, action plans & sustainability** | Data minimisation, consent compliance, data retention (14 months). Shared tracking specification template (event name, description, owner, decisions supported, retention). Define roles. Each team writes a 4-week action plan. Identify digital champions. Schedule 4–6 week check-in. | Addresses adoption and transfer gaps. Compliance/privacy are the \#1 public-sector barrier. Community of practice and “curiosity velocity” are critical. |

# **5\. Common Errors and Prevention Strategies**

| Error | Why it happens | Prevention strategy | Sources |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Treating GA4 like UA** | Mental model stays session/pageview-centric; Category/Action/Label deeply ingrained | Explicit conceptual comparison \+ event mapping exercise from Step 2\. Show that C/A/L is replaced by event names \+ custom params. | McGuirk 2023; Lee et al. 2020 |
| **Logging too many events** | “Collect everything” mindset; no link to decisions | Event design canvas (Step 4\) tied to concrete questions; peer critique of event plans | Lee et al. 2020; Gueye & Exposíto 2024 |
| **Data duplication** | Using GA4 “Create Event” and GTM simultaneously | Explicit warning: use one system for event creation, not both. Standardise on GTM for all custom events. | GA4/GTM best practice |
| **Missing data after setup** | Forgot to click Publish in GTM | Explicitly teach Publish step; require descriptive version names; add to pre-launch checklist | GA4/GTM training literature |
| **Preview Mode not working** | Ad blockers, privacy extensions, or consent banners blocking GTM | Test in Incognito; accept cookies in preview window; disable extensions. Avoid copy-pasting GTM code via Word/Docs (curly quotes break JS). | GA4/GTM training literature |
| **Custom params invisible in reports** | Parameters not registered as Custom Definitions | Step 6 explicitly teaches registration; add to implementation checklist | GA4 documentation |
| **No ownership or governance** | Tool seen as IT’s job only | Define roles and champions in Step 9; align with agility goals; address compliance as \#1 barrier | Atobishi et al. 2024; Wilson & Mergel 2022 |
| **Post-workshop drop-off** | Training not embedded in workflows; no follow-up | Co-designed action plans, follow-up sessions, peer networks, “curiosity velocity” culture (Step 9\) | Selvi et al. 2025; Shibani et al. 2020; Wilson & Mergel 2022 |
| **Over-reliance on vanity metrics** | Dashboards focus on pageviews/sessions without behavioural context | Require each group to define at least one behaviour/change per metric | Sandoval-Ríos et al. 2025; Henderson & Corry 2020 |
| **Ignoring role differences** | Executives feel too simplistic; practitioners feel too vague | Role-specific breakouts \+ reverse mentoring. Executives interpret dashboards; practitioners configure tags. | CDC adult learning; SkillsFuture TAE |

# **6\. Workshop Exemplars**

## **6.1 Campaign tracking for Prompt Royale (form submissions)**

* **Question:** How many citizens register for Prompt Royale, and which channels drive registrations?

* **Scaffold:** Non-coders use GTM’s built-in “Form Submission” trigger rather than custom scripts.

* **Design:** Track form\_id as a parameter to identify if a specific version of the registration form has a high abandonment rate, triggering a UI review.

* **Use:** Weekly GA4 exploration comparing conversion rates by channel; shift spend accordingly.

## **6.2 Measuring impact of Innovation Sparks (link clicks)**

* **Question:** Which product showcases (e.g. Career Kaki) attract the most clicks?

* **Common error:** Using a “Click – All Elements” trigger without a filter causes the tag to fire on background clicks, polluting data.

* **Fix:** Use a “Just Links” trigger filtered so it only fires when Click URL contains the product’s destination path.

## **6.3 Self-service dashboard for a programme team**

* **Question:** Are visitors engaging with key information pages?

* **Design:** Engagement events (scroll, outbound link) for a specific content group.

* **Use:** Simple Looker Studio or GA4 report shared at monthly stand-ups; stakeholders make content changes and track impact.

## **6.4 Suggested 2-day structure**

**Day 1 – GA4 for decision-makers and practitioners.** Morning: Steps 1–3 (frame questions, GA4 mental model \+ architecture, core reports). Afternoon: Step 4 and part of Step 7 (event design canvas, initial explorations). Executive-friendly throughout. \~45% input, \~55% practice.

**Day 2 – GTM \+ implementation \+ sustainability.** Morning: Steps 5–6 (GTM concepts, global config, implement events in sandbox). Afternoon: Steps 7–9 (key events/explorations, testing/debugging/publishing, governance/action plans). Case-based workshop where teams design tracking for a real GovTech campaign. \~35% input, \~65% practice.
